Write-up Section

Black Hat

The visualization misleads through encoding and the legend. The visualization makes it seem groups like the transgender individuals do not exist and focuses mostly on males and females. The axis was used to emphasize the difference between males and females on the force. The graph neglects to mention that men are more likely to be police officers. As a result, they show up more often in allegation lists. This encourages the conclusion that men are worse in this department without this context. The choice of color intentionally highlight this. Null gender complainants were unincluded, which skews the data.

White Hat

The visualization depicts the the counts of allegation categories for civilian complaints against New York Police Officers. A dot plot was chosen to prevent viewers from thinking that there are data points between the years (in the case of a line graph) Brighter colors were chosen for smaller dots to enhance the contrast, which makes them easier to distinguish. The y-axis describes the count of individuals for each complaint type. This was done by counting the shield numbers, which are the unique identifiers for each police officer. The x-axis displays all the years covered by the dataset. Any other manipulation of this axis would not show the full picture regarding the research question. This would be cherrypicking. The size of the dots represent the unique individuals for that year and allegation category. The visualization parameters are set in a way that significant differences between counts are shown through increasing radii of the dots. While someone is interacting with the datapoints, they can see this through the tooltips. This is added to avoid misleading viewers when using total counts of the allegation type, which do not account for the fact that officers can have repeat offenses by itself. For example, in 2020, one of the officers had 3 instances of same complaint type.
